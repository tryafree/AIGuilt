The Impact of Threats to Professional Identity on Guilt in the Use of AI Tools

By Jennifer L. Ziegelmayer and Tabitha L. James | June 9, 2024

In an era where artificial intelligence (AI) is rapidly transforming various professional fields, a groundbreaking study presented at the European Conference on Information Systems (ECIS) 2024 delves into the complex relationship between AI tool usage and professional identity. This TREO (Technology Research, Education, and Opinion) paper, authored by Jennifer L. Ziegelmayer from IESEG School of Management and Tabitha L. James from Virginia Tech, explores how the adoption of AI tools can lead to feelings of guilt among professionals.

Key Findings

  • Professional identity plays a crucial role in how individuals perceive and adopt AI tools in their work.
  • The use of AI tools can sometimes be perceived as a threat to one's professional identity, leading to guilt.
  • The study suggests that the level of guilt experienced may vary depending on the perceived threat to professional identity and the extent of AI tool usage.

Implications for Professionals and Organizations

This research has significant implications for both individual professionals and organizations implementing AI technologies:

  1. Awareness of AI Guilt: Recognizing that feelings of guilt associated with AI use are common and valid can help professionals address these emotions more effectively.
  2. Professional Development: Organizations may need to focus on reframing professional identities to include AI competency, potentially reducing guilt and improving adoption.
  3. Ethical Considerations: The study highlights the need for ongoing discussions about the ethical use of AI in professional settings and its impact on human expertise.

Future Research Directions

The authors suggest several avenues for future research, including:

  • Longitudinal studies to examine how AI guilt evolves over time as professionals become more accustomed to AI tools.
  • Comparative analyses across different professional fields to understand variations in AI guilt and identity threats.
  • Exploration of strategies to mitigate AI guilt while promoting effective AI adoption in professional settings.

As AI continues to reshape the professional landscape, understanding the psychological impacts of its adoption becomes increasingly crucial. This study provides valuable insights into the complex interplay between technology, identity, and professional ethics, paving the way for more nuanced approaches to AI integration in the workplace.

About the Authors

Jennifer L. Ziegelmayer is a researcher at IESEG School of Management, Lille, France. Her work focuses on the intersection of technology, ethics, and professional development.

Tabitha L. James is a professor at Virginia Tech, Blacksburg, VA, USA. She specializes in information systems and their impact on organizational behavior and decision-making.
