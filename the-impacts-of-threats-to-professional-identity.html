The Impact of Threats to Professional Identity on Guilt in the Use of AI Tools

By Jennifer L. Ziegelmayer and Tabitha L. James | Published on June 10, 2023 | Last updated: June 12, 2023

As artificial intelligence (AI) continues to reshape various industries, professionals across different sectors are grappling with the implications of integrating AI tools into their work. A recent study presented at the European Conference on Information Systems (ECIS) 2024 sheds light on the complex relationship between AI tool usage, professional identity, and feelings of guilt.

Research Overview

The TREO (Technology Research, Education, and Opinion) paper titled "The Impact of Threats to Professional Identity on Guilt in the Use of Artificial Intelligence (AI) Tools" was presented by Jennifer L. Ziegelmayer from IESEG School of Management, Lille, France, and Tabitha L. James from Virginia Tech, Blacksburg, VA, USA. This groundbreaking research explores how the use of AI tools can potentially threaten professional identity and lead to feelings of guilt among workers.

Key Findings

  • Professional identity is closely tied to the skills and expertise that define a person's role in their field.
  • The introduction of AI tools can be perceived as a threat to this identity, especially when these tools perform tasks traditionally done by human professionals.
  • This perceived threat can lead to feelings of guilt when using AI tools, as professionals may feel they are "cheating" or devaluing their expertise.
  • The intensity of guilt varies across different professions and depends on how central the automated tasks are to the professional's identity.
"Our research suggests that the relationship between AI tool usage and professional guilt is not straightforward. It's deeply intertwined with how professionals view their role and the value they bring to their work." - Jennifer L. Ziegelmayer

Implications for Different Industries

The study examines the impact of AI tools across various sectors:

1. Creative Industries

In fields like graphic design, writing, and music composition, AI tools that generate content can be seen as particularly threatening to professional identity. Creatives may feel guilty using AI-assisted tools, fearing it diminishes their artistic value.

2. Healthcare

Medical professionals using AI for diagnostics or treatment planning may experience guilt, especially when the AI's recommendations differ from their own judgment. This guilt is often rooted in the ethical responsibility healthcare workers feel towards their patients.

3. Legal Profession

Lawyers using AI for legal research or document analysis might feel that it undermines their expertise in interpreting and applying the law. The guilt here often stems from concerns about the accuracy and comprehensiveness of AI-generated results.

4. Education

Educators utilizing AI for grading or curriculum development may experience guilt related to the perceived devaluation of their pedagogical skills and the personal touch they bring to teaching.

Strategies for Mitigating AI Guilt

The researchers propose several strategies to address and mitigate feelings of guilt associated with AI tool usage:

  1. Reframing AI as an Enhancement: Encourage professionals to view AI tools as enhancers of their skills rather than replacements.
  2. Education and Training: Provide comprehensive training on AI tools to increase understanding and confidence in their use.
  3. Open Dialogue: Foster open discussions about the role of AI in the workplace and its impact on professional identities.
  4. Ethical Guidelines: Develop clear ethical guidelines for AI use in different professions to address concerns about misuse or over-reliance.
  5. Emphasizing Human Value: Highlight the unique human skills that AI cannot replicate, reinforcing the importance of human professionals.

Conclusion

The research by Ziegelmayer and James provides valuable insights into the psychological impact of AI adoption in professional settings. As AI continues to evolve and integrate into various industries, understanding and addressing these feelings of guilt will be crucial for successful and ethical implementation of AI tools.

This study opens up new avenues for research into the psychological aspects of human-AI interaction in the workplace. It also underscores the need for a thoughtful approach to AI integration that considers not just efficiency and productivity, but also the psychological well-being and professional fulfillment of workers.
